History

Gebexa, a captivating board game deeply ingrained in Ethiopian culture, boasts a history that stretches back centuries. Archaeological evidence unearthed at sites like Matara and Yeha reveals the existence of early game boards dating back to the 6th and 7th centuries AD, suggesting a history even further back. These early versions often featured two rows of holes, hinting at the game's evolution over time. While the exact origins remain shrouded in antiquity, these findings provide tangible evidence of Gebexa's deep roots in Ethiopian history.

By the 17th century, Gebexa had become an integral part of Ethiopian social life. References to the game appear in historical texts like the Fetha Nagast, a collection of religious laws, showcasing its presence within both religious and social contexts. Despite occasional religious condemnations, the game flourished among people from all walks of life, from royalty to peasants. Travelers and scholars of the 19th and early 20th centuries, such as Pearce, Harris, and Bent, documented its widespread popularity, describing it as a beloved pastime enjoyed by both men and women. These accounts paint a vivid picture of Gebexa as a central feature of social gatherings, a source of entertainment for both the elite and the common folk. Beyond mere entertainment, Gebexa embodies a unique blend of strategy, skill, and social interaction. The game demands careful planning, strategic thinking, and the ability to anticipate your opponent's moves. It fosters a sense of community, bringing people together for hours of engaging gameplay.
